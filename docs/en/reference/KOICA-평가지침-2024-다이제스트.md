## 1. Evaluation Criteria Framework — DAC 6 + cross-cutting (p.3, p.7)

**Relevance · Coherence · Effectiveness · Efficiency · Impact · Sustainability** + cross-cutting (gender equality, human rights, environment; vulnerable groups, LNOB).

- **Coherence (new)** = internal (synergy with our own government's and KOICA's other projects; conformity with international norms) + external (complementarity, harmonization, and avoidance of duplication with other interventions such as other donors and the partner-country government) (p.27).
- **Composite-score calculation**: only the 5 criteria of Relevance, Coherence, Effectiveness, Efficiency, and **Sustainability readiness** are scored (each 1–4 points) → summed to a maximum of 20 points. **Impact is an ex-post evaluation criterion and is therefore excluded from the Final Evaluation composite score** (p.7).

## 2. ★ Performance Rating Grade Scale (p.7-8) — Core of the System

Introduction: the 2015 Office for Government Policy Coordination "ODA Project Evaluation Grading System"; KOICA subdivided into 6 grades (A–F) in the second half of 2021 (p.7).

### Common 4-point scale definitions (Table 4, p.8)
| Score | Definition |
|:---:|------|
| **1** | Failure to properly implement/consider the majority of project content, with **a clearly negative effect** on achieving results |
| **2** | Failure to properly implement/consider part of the project content, with **some effect** on achieving results |
| **3** | **Achieved what was originally planned to a generally satisfactory degree** |
| **4** | **Achieved everything intended** and also confirmed **results beyond expectations** |

### Composite evaluation grade (Table 3, p.8)
| Composite score (out of 20) | KOICA grade | Office for Government Policy Coordination grade |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| 18 or higher | **A** | Highly successful |
| 16 to below 18 | **B** | Successful |
| 14 to below 16 | **C** | Successful |
| 12 to below 14 | **D** | Partially successful |
| 10 to below 12 | **E** | Partially successful |
| Below 10 | **F** | Unsatisfactory |

> The evaluation team bears responsibility for carefully checking that the narrative text and the assigned grade do not contradict each other (p.7). → A check item for the verifier.

## 3. ★ Effectiveness — Definition, Questions, Rubric (p.9·12·27)

**Definition (p.27, project's own summary):** Looks at whether the project actually achieved the objectives it set — judging together the extent to which objectives/results were achieved (or are expected to be achieved) and how results were distributed across groups. The core phrase of the standard OECD-DAC definition (Effectiveness) is *"the extent to which the intervention achieved, or is expected to achieve, its objectives"* (OECD-DAC).

**3 core questions (p.27, p.11):**
1. Have the project's direct/primary results (outputs) been achieved, or are they expected to be achieved, as planned?
2. Has the project achieved results equitably by **including socially marginalized groups**? (LNOB)
3. (For international-organization projects) Has the **visibility** of KOICA/Korean-government support been effectively secured?

**Effectiveness 4-point rubric (output-based, p.9):**
| Score | Definition |
|:---:|------|
| 1 | One or more outputs significantly unachieved + the unavoidability/validity of the reasons is not recognized, or the likelihood of achieving mid-to-long-term results is very low |
| 2 | One or more outputs partly unachieved + reasons partly recognized, but a high likelihood of a negative effect on achieving results/objectives |
| 3 | Most outputs achieved; some fall short, but the reasons are recognized as valid and can be overcome |
| 4 | All outputs in the final PDM achieved at 100% or more + high quality, with a high likelihood of contributing to mid-to-long-term results/objectives |

**Points to note (p.27):** ① If objectives/results are unclear, **re-establish the results model (ePDM)** before evaluating; ② Confirm differentiated results (vulnerable groups); ③ **Analyze enabling/disabling factors** (internal resources such as finance, personnel, and regulations; output quality-check protocols; management of external contextual change).

## 4. Pool of Standard Evaluation Questions for the 6 Criteria (p.26-28, Table 17)

- **Relevance**: Was the design shaped to reflect stakeholder demand/priorities? / Was the design appropriately managed against changing circumstances? / (Partnership) Was the partnership pursued appropriately? / (Follow-on) Was the design shaped taking prior projects into account?
- **Coherence**: (Internal) Complementarity, harmonization, and added value with the Korean government's and KOICA's other projects? / (External) Complementarity and harmonization with other donors, the partner country, etc.? / (International organization) Linkage and synergy within the programme?
- **Efficiency**: Economical and timely? / Efficient balance and interaction between inputs and activities? / (Partnership) Efficient management of partnership and communication?
- **Sustainability**: Financial/economic self-reliance capacity and crisis response? / Institutional and social environment in place?
- **Vulnerable groups / human rights**: Design that identifies vulnerable groups and reflects their needs? / Resolution of factors limiting participation and information sharing? / Guaranteeing non-discriminatory results through disaggregated-data monitoring?
- **Impact** (ex-post evaluation): Achievement/likelihood of long-term, broad, transformative effect? (p.10·12)

## 5. ★ Evaluation-Report Quality-Review System (p.15-17) — Quality Inspector's Rubric (2024 revised edition)

**The 2017 version's 5 items / 80 points is abolished.** New system = 100 points by section + A–D grades, with **a 100%-independent evaluation panel and blind review**.

### Section point allocation (Table 8, p.15)
| Section | Points |
|------|:---:|
| Ⅰ. Evaluation design and methodology (context analysis 10 + rigor 10) | 20 |
| Ⅱ. Reliability of data | 20 |
| Ⅲ. Analysis and evaluation results | 25 |
| Ⅳ. Conclusions (validity of conclusions/recommendations) | 20 |
| Ⅴ. Report structure and format | 10 |

### Quality grade (Table 9, p.15)
| Grade | Score | Result |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| A (Excellent) | 90↑ | Pass |
| B (Good) | 80~90 | Pass |
| C (Fair) | 70~80 | Pass |
| **D (Poor)** | Below 70 | **Non-Pass** (one round of supplementation and re-review; if D again, the evaluation grade is deleted) |

- **24 detailed questions** (Table 10, p.16-17): each rated Excellent (10) / Good (8) / Somewhat poor (6) / Poor (4). For example, Q13 asks whether the degree of project-results achievement was re-verified by objective and reliable means, and Q18 asks whether the final evaluation grade matches the narrative text. → Transplanted verbatim as the quality-inspector agent's rubric.

## 6. 8 Guiding Principles of Evaluation Ethics (Table 12, p.19) — AI Rules

Independence · Impartiality · Credibility · Accountability · Accuracy · **Completeness** (strengths and weaknesses together) · Transparency · Protection of evaluation participants (anonymity, minimizing negative effects).

- No assertion of unverified information (accuracy), balance of strengths and weaknesses (completeness), anonymization (participant protection), clear statement of sources/evidence (credibility).
- Evaluatees, too, are subject to mutual evaluation of their ethics compliance (prohibitions on interfering with the evaluation, demanding adjustment of results, etc.) (Table 13, p.20).

## 7. Evaluation Procedure (4 stages, p.22)

**① Evaluation planning → ② Evaluation implementation → ③ Submission of evaluation results → ④ Evaluation feedback** (reorganized from the 5 stages of 2017). New: Theory of Change (ToC) analysis (p.3-4), remote/non-face-to-face surveys (p.43), final review meeting.
